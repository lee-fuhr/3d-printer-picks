Frequently asked questions

What type of 3D printer should I buy: FDM or resin?

FDM (filament) printers are the right starting point for most people. They print in plastic filament, produce durable functional parts, have a more forgiving workflow, and work in any room without special ventilation. Resin printers produce far finer detail and smoother surfaces, which makes them the right choice for miniatures, jewelry casting, and dental or engineering prototypes. The trade-off is a messier workflow: liquid resin requires gloves, ventilation, an isopropyl alcohol wash station, and a UV curing lamp. If you want to print boxes, brackets, phone cases, or toys: FDM. If you want to print tiny detailed figures or precision parts: resin.

How much does a good 3D printer cost?

A capable beginner FDM printer runs $200–$350 in 2026. At that price, machines like the Creality Ender 3 V3 or Bambu Lab A1 Mini ship with auto bed leveling and enough quality control to produce good prints reliably. Mid-range FDM printers in the $400–$700 range add speed, enclosures for engineering materials, or multi-color capability. Resin printers start around $200 for a small-format MSLA machine, but the ongoing cost of resin ($40–$80 per liter) and consumables (FEP films, gloves, wash solution) adds up faster than FDM filament ($20–$30 per kilogram spool).

Do 3D printers require a lot of maintenance?

Modern printers require considerably less maintenance than machines from five years ago, but the’e not zero-effort appliances. FDM printers need occasional bed leveling checks (though most modern machines do this automatically), nozzle cleaning or replacement every few hundred hours of use, and lubrication of the motion system every few months. Resin printers need FEP film replacement when it becomes cloudy or punctured, typically every 2–5 liters of resin, and careful cleaning of the resin vat after each session. The biggest maintenance variable is brand quality control: Prusa and Bambu Lab have better out-of-the-box experience than older Creality or Anycubic machines.

What materials can I print with?

PLA is the default starting material for FDM printers: easy to print, minimal warping, biodegradable, and available in hundreds of colors. PETG adds flexibility, moisture resistance, and better heat tolerance without much added difficulty. ABS and ASA are tougher and more heat-resistant but warp easily and need an enclosure and good ventilation. TPU is a flexible rubber-like material used for phone cases, gaskets, and wearables, requiring a direct-drive extruder and patience. Engineering materials like PA (nylon), PC (polycarbonate), and carbon-fiber composites need a high-temperature hotend and an enclosure. If yo’e starting out, spend your first month on PLA before experimenting with anything else.

How long does it take to print something?

Print time depends on the objec’ size, the layer height you choose, and the printe’ maximum speed. A small phone stand or figurine at standard quality might take 1–2 hours on a fast modern printer. A medium-sized object like a camera grip or a replacement knob takes 3–8 hours. Large items can run 12–24 hours or more. High-speed printers like the Bambu Lab P1S cut those times dramatically, sometimes by 60–70% compared to older Creality or Prusa machines running at default speeds. Resin printers are paradoxically faster at detail work: print time scales with height, not volume, so a tray full of small figurines takes the same time as one.
