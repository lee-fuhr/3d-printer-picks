Resin 3D printers excel at capturing fine details, making them the go-to choice for miniature wargaming figures, character models, and precision engineering prototypes. Unlike filament printers, they cure liquid resin layer by layer, delivering smooth surfaces and sharp edges that need little post-processing. When choosing, focus on build volume (larger lets you print bigger objects or more parts at once), print resolution (XY and Z-axis capability), and any convenience features like auto-leveling or air filtration. Budget is another major factor: entry-level resin printers are now very affordable, but stepping up to a larger format printer costs more and takes up more desk space. Frequently asked questions

Is resin 3D printing safe?

Resin is a skin and respiratory irritant and should be handled with nitrile gloves every time you touch it in liquid form. Proper ventilation is important: print in a space with good airflow or use an air purifier with an activated carbon filter. The cured (hardened) resin after washing and UV curing is generally safe to handle, but uncured liquid resin on printed surfaces before the wash cycle is not. Dedicated resin printers have sealed chambers that contain odors reasonably well, but outdoor or garage printing is a common choice for people who print frequently or in large volumes.

Wha’ the difference between MSLA and DLP resin printers?

MSLA (masked stereolithography) printers use an LCD screen as a mask and a UV LED array behind it to cure an entire layer at once. Print time per layer is constant regardless of how many objects are on the build plate: a full tray takes the same time as one figure. DLP (digital light processing) printers use a projector that can warp at the edges of larger build plates. For most consumers, MSLA is the dominant technology and the distinction is largely academic when comparing modern machines from Anycubic or Elegoo. Both produce excellent detail at the consumer level.

How do you wash and cure resin prints?

After printing, the model is still coated in uncured liquid resin. You need to wash it in isopropyl alcohol (90%+) or a dedicated resin wash solution, typically using an ultrasonic washer or a wash station that agitates the liquid around the part. After washing, the part needs UV curing , usually 2–10 minutes under a dedicated UV curing lamp, depending on the resin type and part thickness. All-in-one wash-and-cure stations simplify this process significantly and are worth buying alongside your first resin printer. The wash-and-cure step is the main reason resin printing has a higher workflow overhead than FDM.
