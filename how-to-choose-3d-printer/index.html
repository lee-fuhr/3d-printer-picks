How to choose a 3D printer

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

Choosing a 3D printer starts with three honest questions: what do you want to make, how much do you want to tinker, and what’s your budget. Most hobbyists will do just fine with a well-reviewed FDM printer under a few hundred dollars, while resin printers shine for tiny, detailed miniatures. Skip the marketing specs and focus on build volume, bed leveling type, and extruder design.

What do you want to make, how much tinkering, and what’s your budget?

Before scrolling through spec sheets, answer three questions. First, what do you plan to print? Functional parts, cosplay props, and simple toys call for an FDM printer. Miniatures, jewelry, and highly detailed models are better suited to a resin printer. Second, how much do you want to tinker? Some printers arrive nearly ready to run; others require constant calibration and part upgrades. Third, what’s your budget? Entry-level FDM printers are very affordable, while enthusiast-grade machines with enclosures and advanced features cost considerably more. These three answers will narrow your choices faster than any feature list. If you want to print replacement clips and garden pots without fuss, a mid-range pre-assembled FDM printer is the smartest path. If you’re looking to produce smooth tabletop figures and don’t mind post-processing, a resin machine will make you happier.

FDM vs resin: which one is right for you?

FDM (fused deposition modeling) printers melt a plastic filament and lay it down in layers. They are the workhorses of the 3D printing world: cheaper, easier to scale up, and compatible with a wide range of sturdy materials like PLA, PETG, and TPU. They produce parts with visible layer lines, which can be smoothed with sanding, but they are generally stronger and more forgiving for functional prototypes. Resin printers use a vat of liquid photopolymer and a UV light source to cure each layer. They achieve much finer detail, often down to a layer height that is barely visible to the naked eye. The trade-offs are higher cost per print, messy cleanup, and strong fumes that require good ventilation. If you want ultra-smooth, small objects with crisp detail, resin is the way to go. For everything else, FDM is the more practical, less fussy choice.

The specs that actually matter (and the ones that are just marketing)

Focus on build volume, layer resolution, bed leveling type, and extruder design. Build volume defines the maximum size of objects you can print. A common range for a starter printer is about the size of a shoebox, which suits most home projects. Layer resolution on an FDM printer typically runs from 0.1 to 0.3 mm for standard prints; chasing sub-0.05 mm layers is rarely necessary and slows print times dramatically. A direct-drive extruder handles flexible materials better than a Bowden setup, but Bowden setups are lighter and can print faster. Ignore inflated maximum temperature numbers unless you plan to print engineering-grade materials like polycarbonate. Also ignore the number of preloaded print profiles or presets, they don’t guarantee quality. Bed leveling is the spec to watch: automatic mesh bed leveling saves you a lot of frustration, while fully manual leveling is a skill you’ll spend time learning.

When do you need an enclosure?

An enclosure is essential if you plan to print materials like ABS, ASA, or nylon, which warp without a stable, warm chamber. It also helps reduce drafts and improves layer adhesion for those materials. For PLA, the most common filament, an enclosure is unnecessary and can actually cause overheating if poorly ventilated. Many mid-range and enthusiast printers come with built-in enclosures or offer them as add-ons. If you stick with PLA and PETG, you can skip the enclosure without worry. But if you eventually want to print stronger or heat-resistant parts, an enclosed machine will save you frustration. Open-frame printers are fine for most beginners.

Budget guide: starter, mid-range, and enthusiast tiers

In the budget-friendly starter tier, you’ll find well-reviewed FDM printers that offer a solid build volume, automatic bed leveling, and reliable performance. These machines are perfect for learning the basics and printing PLA. Expect to do some assembly, but not a full kit. The mid-range tier costs a few hundred dollars more and typically adds a larger build volume, a direct-drive extruder, a heated bed that heats faster, and often a partially enclosed design. These printers handle a wider range of materials and deliver more consistent results without constant tweaking. Enthusiast-tier machines cost more than that and include features like fully enclosed chambers, multi-material capabilities, precise linear rails, and excellent technical support. If you’re a designer or engineer printing prototypes daily, this tier saves time and reduces failed prints. For most home users, the mid-range tier offers the best balance of value and capability.

Why the community and firmware matter

A 3D printer is only as good as its support network. Machines backed by active user communities, official forums, and open-source firmware like Marlin or Klipper are far easier to troubleshoot and upgrade. You’ll find printable mods, detailed guides, and help with weird calibration issues. A printer with closed, proprietary firmware and a small user base can become a brick if the manufacturer loses interest. Before buying, search for the printer model plus words like ‘issues’ or ‘mod’. If you see a thriving community of owners sharing tips and files, that’s a good sign. For first-time buyers, a printer that ships with pre-configured firmware but allows manual tuning later is ideal. Avoid kits that require significant soldering or custom firmware flashing unless you enjoy that tinkering.

Frequently asked questions

Is a larger build volume always better?

Not necessarily. Larger build volumes often mean bigger, slower printers and higher costs. For most hobbyists, a build volume big enough for a helmet or a vase is plenty. Larger parts can be split and glued.

What layer height should I use for everyday prints?

For most FDM prints, a layer height between 0.2 and 0.3 mm gives a good balance of speed and appearance. Use 0.12 mm for finer detail on small objects, and 0.3 mm for quick prototypes that don’t need smooth surfaces.

Do I need a heated bed?

Yes, for anything beyond PLA. A heated bed helps prevent warping on PETG, ABS, and other materials. Most modern FDM printers include one; if a budget printer lacks it, stick strictly to PLA.

Can I print metal or carbon fiber with a consumer 3D printer?

You can print metal- or carbon-fiber-filled filaments, but they require a hardened steel nozzle because standard brass nozzles wear out quickly. Even then, the final part is not solid metal, it’s a composite and requires post-processing to remove the binder.

How noisy are 3D printers?

Entry-level FDM printers are moderately noisy due to fans and stepper motors. You can reduce noise with rubber dampeners, a concrete paver base, or adding acoustic panels. Resin printers are generally quieter because they have fewer moving parts, though the UV curing light’s fan can still hum.

Should I buy a kit or a pre-assembled printer?

Buy a kit only if you enjoy building and troubleshooting. Kits are often cheaper but require hours of assembly and calibration. Pre-assembled printers cost a little more but save time and frustration, especially for first-time users. If your budget allows, start pre-assembled.

In shortThe best 3D printer for you is the one that matches what you actually want to make and how much patience you bring to the table. Stick with a well-supported, mid-range FDM printer for general use, and only go resin if tiny details are non-negotiable. Ignore hype, read real owner experiences, and remember that the community matters more than the specs sheet.
